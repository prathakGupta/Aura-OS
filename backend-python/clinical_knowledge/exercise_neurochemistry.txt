SECTION: The Revolution — Exercise as Brain Medicine

Dr. John J. Ratey, associate clinical professor of psychiatry at Harvard Medical School, has dedicated his career to studying the transformative effects of exercise on the brain. His research shows that physical activity can reduce stress, sharpen thinking, lift mood, and boost memory.

Exercise helps increase the ability to regulate emotions. It improves neuroplasticity — the environment for brain cells to grow. The only way we learn anything is for our brain cells to grow. The best way known — with any activity, drug, or supplement — to increase brain cells' ability to grow is through exercise.

What we see is an improvement across the board:
- Ability to regulate emotions (decrease in discipline problems)
- Increase in participation and attendance
- Increase in grades, test scores, and classroom success
- Better executive function including working memory, sorting, sequencing, imagining, and consequence evaluation

---

SECTION: BDNF — Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor

Exercise triggers the release of BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor), which Dr. Ratey calls "Miracle-Gro for the brain." BDNF:
- Promotes the growth of new neurons (neurogenesis), particularly in the hippocampus (memory and learning center)
- Strengthens existing neural connections (synaptic plasticity)
- Protects neurons from stress-induced damage
- Is reduced in depression, anxiety, and ADHD — exercise restores BDNF levels

The hippocampus, which is critical for memory formation and emotional regulation, physically shrinks in chronic stress and depression. Exercise-induced BDNF can reverse this shrinkage.

SECTION: Exercise and ADHD — Complex Motor Movements

For ADHD, the key neurochemical deficits are dopamine and norepinephrine — the same neurotransmitters targeted by ADHD medications like Adderall and Ritalin. Exercise naturally increases both.

RECOMMENDED EXERCISE TYPES FOR ADHD:
1. Complex motor movements (martial arts, rock climbing, gymnastics, dance):
   - These require intense focus on body position, sequencing, and timing
   - They engage the cerebellum and prefrontal cortex simultaneously
   - The "dual task" nature prevents mind-wandering — you can't practice a roundhouse kick while thinking about your inbox
   - Martial arts are particularly effective because they also teach self-regulation, impulse control, and respect for structure

2. Team sports with complex strategy (basketball, soccer):
   - Require constant tactical decision-making (executive function training)
   - Social bonding releases oxytocin, reducing isolation common in ADHD

3. High-intensity interval training (HIIT):
   - Produces the largest spike in dopamine and norepinephrine
   - 20-30 minutes of HIIT can provide 2-3 hours of improved focus
   - Mimics the pharmacological effect of stimulant medication

NEUROCHEMICAL MECHANISM FOR ADHD:
- Dopamine: Exercise increases dopamine synthesis and release, improving motivation, reward processing, and sustained attention
- Norepinephrine: Exercise elevates norepinephrine, improving alertness, arousal, and the ability to filter distractions
- Combined effect: 30 minutes of vigorous exercise produces focus benefits equivalent to a low dose of methylphenidate (Ritalin)

SECTION: Exercise and Anxiety — Zone 2 Cardio and Yoga

For anxiety, the neurochemical targets are different: the goal is to increase GABA (the brain's primary calming neurotransmitter) and lower cortisol (the stress hormone).

RECOMMENDED EXERCISE TYPES FOR ANXIETY:
1. Zone 2 cardio (moderate-intensity sustained movement):
   - Walking, jogging, cycling, swimming at a pace where you can hold a conversation
   - Heart rate: 60-70% of maximum
   - Duration: 30-45 minutes minimum
   - This activates the parasympathetic nervous system ("rest and digest")
   - Rhythmic, repetitive motion is inherently calming to the nervous system
   - Produces the largest sustained GABA increase

2. Yoga and mindful movement:
   - Combines physical postures with breath control and meditation
   - Directly stimulates the vagus nerve (parasympathetic activation)
   - Has been shown to increase GABA levels by up to 27% after a single session (compared to reading, which produced no GABA increase)
   - Specific poses: inversions, forward folds, and supported reclining poses activate the relaxation response
   - Yoga Nidra (body scan meditation): shown to reduce cortisol by 23% in clinical trials

3. Tai Chi and Qigong:
   - Slow, deliberate movements with deep breathing
   - Particularly effective for older adults and those with physical limitations
   - Reduces cortisol and increases GABA and serotonin

NEUROCHEMICAL MECHANISM FOR ANXIETY:
- GABA: Exercise increases GABA production, providing a natural anti-anxiety effect similar to benzodiazepines — but without dependence or cognitive impairment
- Cortisol regulation: Regular moderate exercise lowers baseline cortisol levels and improves the HPA axis's ability to return to baseline after stress
- Endorphins: Beta-endorphins produce feelings of well-being and reduce pain perception
- Serotonin: Aerobic exercise increases tryptophan availability in the brain, boosting serotonin synthesis

SECTION: Exercise and Depression — Rebuilding Dopamine Circuits

For depression, the primary targets are serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine — the "monoamine" neurotransmitters that are deficient in depressive states.

RECOMMENDED EXERCISE TYPES FOR DEPRESSION:
1. Aerobic exercise (running, cycling, dancing):
   - 30 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise, 3-5x per week
   - A landmark Duke University study showed exercise was as effective as sertraline (Zoloft) for major depression
   - The effect was dose-dependent: more sessions = lower relapse rate

2. Resistance training (weightlifting, bodyweight exercises):
   - A 2018 meta-analysis found strength training significantly reduces depression symptoms
   - The sense of progressive overload (gradually lifting more) rebuilds self-efficacy
   - Hormonal response: increases testosterone and growth hormone, both of which are mood-elevating

3. Group exercise classes:
   - Combines physical activity with social connection
   - Reduces isolation, which is a major driver of depressive episodes
   - The accountability factor helps overcome the "can't get started" paralysis

NEUROCHEMICAL MECHANISM FOR DEPRESSION:
- Serotonin: Exercise increases serotonin synthesis by improving tryptophan transport across the blood-brain barrier
- Dopamine: Regular exercise upregulates dopamine receptors, making the brain more responsive to natural rewards
- Norepinephrine: Exercise-induced norepinephrine improves energy and alertness, countering the fatigue of depression
- Endocannabinoids: The "runner's high" is now attributed to endocannabinoids (anandamide), not just endorphins

SECTION: Exercise Timing and Duration

OPTIMAL TIMING:
- Morning exercise: Best for anxiety (cortisol is naturally highest in AM; exercise helps channel it)
- Afternoon exercise (2-4 PM): Best for ADHD (body temperature peaks, reaction time is fastest)
- The ideal school day starts later and includes an episode of activity
- Exercise doesn't have to be first period — benefits come from activity distributed throughout the day

MINIMUM EFFECTIVE DOSE:
- For mood benefits: 20 minutes of moderate activity
- For cognitive benefits (focus, memory): 30 minutes of vigorous activity
- For BDNF increase: 20-40 minutes of continuous aerobic activity
- The effect lasts 2-4 hours post-exercise, providing a window of enhanced cognitive function

SECTION: The Naperville Study — Evidence from Schools

Naperville School District 203 implemented a fitness-based physical education program:
- Only 3% of their children were overweight (vs. national average of 30%+)
- Students took the international TIMSS science and math test
- Results: #1 in the world in science, #6 in the world in math
- The program focused on fitness for ALL students, not just athletes
- The "low-hanging fruit" — non-athletic, sedentary kids — got the biggest benefits

Key insight: It's not about Friday Night Lights (athlete-focused PE). It's about getting ALL kids as fit as possible. This leads to better citizens, better students, and healthier individuals.

SECTION: Current Mental Health Crisis and Exercise

30% or more of children in schools have:
- Generalized anxiety disorder
- Attention deficit disorder
- Depression
- Higher incidences of suicide

All of these can be helped enormously by a good physical education program as well as adequate time for recess. The more fit a person is, the better they deal with stress.
